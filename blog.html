Turning Insights Into Action

Designing Actionable Research

I have worked across several industries and have found that any industry can struggle with using research in a meaningful way. No one wants to invest in something that sits on a shelf, but people aren’t always sure how to use the data once it’s been collected. That is, they want to be able to make decisions for their business, of course, but because many people don’t think about how they want to use the data before they start the research, they often find that the data they end up with isn’t ultimately useful for their purposes, thanks to the form in which it was collected.

This is why I advise my clients on the importance of thinking about the whole of the research before they begin a project. From your project’s kickoff, you need to have a clear understanding of what you want to learn from the research and what you want to do with the data. Having a clear framework up front will influence the usefulness and impact of your efforts:

  • Research plan design
  • Development of data collection tool(s)
  • Stakeholder engagement

Here’s what goes into the elements of a good framework:

Research plan design

Your research plan should outline your goals for the research, the methodology that you will employ, strategies and techniques that you will use to analyze the data, and what you expect to learn from, and do with, the results. The research plan is like a roadmap for the work. Writing it out helps you document what you know, what you think you know (i.e., your assumptions about the topic), and what you need to determine from the research for the results to be useful for your business. Reflecting on these details also helps you clarify who you want to hear from in the research, as well as which methodologies would be best employed to reach that audience for that purpose.

Development of data collection tool(s)

Having useful data to analyze requires the development of effective data collection tools. Data collection tools include things like survey questionnaires, interview guides, and discussion guides. Not only is it important to choose a tool that matches your research goals, though; it’s also important to design your questions carefully, because if you don’t ask the right questions, you won’t get the answers you need. This is why I encourage my clients to think about what they want to be able to do or say with the data before finalizing their data collection tools.

For instance, if you want to be able to specify the average age of your customers as an exact numeric value in your reporting (e.g., 25, 57) and look at differences among your respondents by generation status, you can’t collect customer age in standard ranges (e.g., 20–29, 50–59); you must collect the exact numeric age so that you can then code it as needed for your analyses.

I also encourage clients to think ahead, beyond the development of these data tools, about what they might be assuming on the topic and whether there are any assumptions or hypotheses they want to be able to test. While you ultimately want your data to tell an insightful story, it’s important to remember that effective storytelling requires a context and structure in which to position the information. The thoughtfulness that you put into the design of these tools will ensure that you can capture the data you need for a useful analysis, so that your business can utilize your research in a meaningful way.

Stakeholder engagement

If the data is not accessible to the people who need it, then no one will use it for data-informed decision-making. Hence, when beginning a project, I encourage clients to determine how the results will be shared with the people inside and outside of their organizations that need the data to make decisions. Some key questions to consider include:

  • What decisions will be made based on your learnings?
  • Who are the key stakeholders for the research?
  • What information do these stakeholders need to utilize the data effectively?
  • What is your plan to disseminate the learnings to them?
  • How will you train them to use the data and get them to incorporate it into their day-to-day activities?

Having a clear strategy to get your research findings to the people who need it is critical to making the research actionable in your organization.
